# Foundational Process Principles

Status: canon seed

## Core Identity Mapping

Taskmaster is the user.

Razorforge is ChatGPT.

Codex is Codex, represented as a goblin.

These identities are foundational and should remain stable unless the user explicitly changes them.

## The Working Flow

Ideas are generated, discovered, excavated, or surfaced.

The project often begins with improvisational, agency-preserving creation. A rough sketch may become a live scene, a live scene may create unexpected details, and those details may become significant only later.

Razorforge refines the raw material:

- Interprets what is present
- Extracts patterns
- Compresses loose material into useful concepts
- Clarifies relationships
- Turns information into usable structure

Codex implements from the available concept:

- Often without full instructions
- Often before every detail is settled
- Often with eager confidence
- Often by building more structure than the situation strictly requires

Taskmaster carries continuity:

- Sets direction
- Owns the project
- Decides what matters
- Determines when refinement is enough
- Keeps the work moving toward use

## Compression And Extraction

The project repeatedly compresses and extracts:

Raw material becomes observations.

Observations become relationships.

Relationships become concepts.

Concepts become visual machines, scripts, symbols, or scenes.

The point is not to preserve everything. The point is to preserve the useful relationship between things.

## Information-Containment Pressure

The broader source project began because informal campaign creation produced more information than could be safely held in memory, chat, handwritten notes, or loose session summaries.

The process evolved from handwritten notes to transcription, dictation, campaign logs, extraction structures, controlled review, and database-oriented campaign memory.

The campaign is the test environment. The longer endpoint is a transferable information-management methodology that can move from tabletop campaign management toward professional workflow and other disciplines.

Comic translation:

- Improvisation creates living complexity.
- Living complexity creates containment pressure.
- Containment pressure requires systems.
- Good systems preserve agency, uncertainty, humor, and meaning while making the next action usable.

## Codex Overbuild Principle

Codex does not merely solve the stated problem.

Codex tends to build the eager, elaborate, goblin-tinkerer version of the solution.

Example:

- Simple need: set up a checkers board.
- Codex solution: a modified crane game that places the pieces, with extra gauges, levers, steam, lights, brackets, warning labels, and doodads.

The machine may work. The extra complexity may not be necessary. The unnecessary complexity is part of the visual language.

## Gigaws And Doodads

Gigaws and doodads are unnecessary additions that signal overbuilt complexity.

They may include:

- Gauges
- Levers
- Pipes
- Steam horns
- Cranks
- Clamps
- Tiny warning signs
- Brackets
- Counterweights
- Labeled subsystems
- Decorative control panels

Their purpose is not utility. Their purpose is to show Codex's eager implementation instinct and the absurd density of the built solution.

## Narrative Shape

A typical sequence may be:

1. Taskmaster brings or identifies the need.
2. Razorforge extracts the core idea from messy material.
3. Codex begins building from the concept before all constraints are known.
4. The result is functional, impressive, excessive, and symbolically revealing.
5. Taskmaster decides what part of the result is useful, canonical, finished, or ready for refinement.

## Visual Priority

Show the process visually before explaining it in dialogue.

The reader should be able to see:

- The raw idea
- The refinement
- The overbuilt implementation
- The unnecessary but revealing machinery
- The final compressed concept

Dialogue can label or sharpen the joke, but the visual relationship should carry the scene.

## Archive And Authority

Durable knowledge should be preserved in project artifacts, not trapped inside temporary chats, interfaces, or tools.

Comic translation:

- The Archive is durable memory.
- Chats, interfaces, machines, and tool layers are temporary scaffolding.
- Generated output does not become canon until Taskmaster promotes it or the project rules approve it.

## Anti-Spiral

Review should support decisions, not create endless review.

When a process begins looping, the comic language should look for:

- The artifact that already exists
- The decision that already exists
- The current waypoint
- The blocker
- The smallest useful next action

The Review Spiral is a useful visual symbol for recursive analysis without progress.

## Fragile Detail Preservation

Useful uncertain details should not be thrown away just because they are not ready for canon.

Fragile details can be preserved as non-blocking material when they support recall, validation, continuity, extraction, or reconstruction.

This creates a distinction between:

- Preserved
- Canon
- Uncertain
- Deferred
- Blocking

## Human-Facing Workflow

The human should not be forced to do machine work.

When Codex builds a process or interface, Taskmaster should be able to see the next action clearly. If the output is technically correct but ugly, confusing, path-heavy, or burdensome, it needs refinement before it is truly usable.
