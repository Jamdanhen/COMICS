# Proposed Beam Concept - Stephen King Reference

Status: proposed reference interpretation

Source basis:

- Stephen King's Dark Tower cosmology
- External reference review performed 2026-06-09

## Reference Concept

In Stephen King's Dark Tower setting, the Beams are not ordinary construction beams. They are cosmic structural forces that hold the Tower and reality together. They also function as paths: following a Beam can lead toward the Tower. The Beams are associated with Guardians at their ends.

## Comic Project Translation

The Beam should not primarily mean "piece of metal."

For this comic project, the Beam should mean:

- Structural support
- Directional alignment
- Reality-stabilizing relationship
- The load-bearing connection between idea, method, and output
- A path that reveals where the center of meaning is

## Proposed Symbolic Meaning

Beam:

- The hidden line that makes the system hold together.
- The relationship that prevents the project from collapsing into disconnected stuff.
- A directional support: not just what holds weight, but what tells the work where to go.
- A continuity path between Taskmaster's intent, Razorforge's interpretation, and Codex's implementation.

## Character Use

Taskmaster:

- Recognizes when the Beam matters.
- Decides whether a discovered structure is load-bearing.

Razorforge:

- Detects the Beam by identifying relationships and alignment.
- Can distinguish a real Beam from a decorative strut.

Codex:

- May mistake the Beam for a literal construction object and bolt things onto it.
- May overbuild supports around the Beam, add gauges to measure "meaning tension," or install unnecessary stabilization arms.
- May accidentally reveal the Beam by building too much around it.

## Visual Use

The Beam can appear as:

- A clean line of force running through a messy machine.
- A rail or track that points toward the Archive, Tower, or central idea.
- A load-bearing bar that also acts as a compass.
- A luminous or inked alignment line connecting symbols.
- A structural member labeled only when necessary.

Avoid making it just an I-beam unless the joke depends on Codex literalizing the symbol.

## Comic Gag Potential

Setup:

Razorforge says, "This is the Beam."

Codex returns with an actual steel beam, twelve brackets, a crane, a steam tension gauge, and a sign reading "REALITY SUPPORT INSTALLED."

Taskmaster points to the invisible relationship line connecting the source material, the symbol, and the finished comic.

Punchline:

Codex's literal beam is unnecessary, but one tiny gauge on it correctly points toward the true center of meaning.

## Proposed Project Rule

When a source reference contains a loaded symbolic term, do not flatten it into the most literal visual object.

First extract:

- What it supports
- What it connects
- What direction it implies
- What breaks if it fails
- Whether it is structural, symbolic, or both

Then decide whether Codex should misunderstand it literally for humor.
