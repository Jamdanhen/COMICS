## Assessment Rule

Strong comic targets should have at least three of these traits:

- visual action
- recurring symbol potential
- character or institutional behavior
- a clear joke without heavy exposition
- connection to current approved pages
- source/canon tension
- meaning-preservation value

Avoid targets that are only abstract governance unless they can be staged as an object, place, notice, inspector, map, shelf, mine, or machine.

## Strongest Near-Term Targets

### 1. The Mine / Excavation Mine

Priority: very high

Corrected meaning:

The Mine is an excavation mine, not the naval mine symbol. It is a place where digging, discovery, rare ores, source fragments, and ideas emerge.

Comic use:

- Taskmaster enters a working mine beneath Around Here.
- Razorforge identifies a vein or seam.
- Codex builds an excessive extraction tool.
- A strange ore chunk becomes an idea, symbol, or usable bit.

Why it works:

It visualizes extraction without needing labels. It connects directly to idea generation, repository work, and the lunch bench origin.

Risk:

Too many labels can turn it into a glossary page. Keep it visual and physical.

### 2. The Bench Outside The Mine

Priority: very high

Meaning:

A work-adjacent conversation space where meaning accumulates during the pause.

Comic use:

- Taskmaster and Razorforge pause outside the mine.
- A lunchbox, ham sandwich, map, and mined ore sit nearby.

### 3. Chief Inspector

Priority: high

Source snapshot:

A real crab with a fake mustache investigates category fraud.

Core meaning:

The mustache is not decoration. It marks imitation, false identity, and category trouble.

Comic use:

- The Chief Inspector reviews category-fraud cases after the Most Wanted board.
- He inspects imitation crab, boneless wing, or a suspect label.
- The fake mustache itself becomes evidence.

Why it works:

It directly follows `Around Here's Most Wanted` and gives Category Fraud Division a character without over-explaining the bureaucracy.

Risk:

Could become too cartoony if drawn as pure mascot. Keep it as dead-serious inspector humor.

### 4. Biological Committee Notice

Priority: high

Meaning:

Sleep, hunger, time, and physical limits issue violations/reminders.

Comic use:

- A project is in progress.
- A formal notice arrives from the Biological Committee.
- It cites hunger, sleep debt, or time as jurisdictional violations.

Why it works:

It turns real physical limits into sincere institutional humor. It is immediately relatable and visually simple.

Risk:

Should not make the body an enemy. The committee is a real pressure, not villainy.

### 5. Around Here Chronicle / Monocle / Gazelle

Priority: high

Meaning:

Around Here has an in-world newspaper, focused articles, and a gazelle reporter born from Gazette/Gazelle drift.

Comic use:
