## Core Snapshot

The Chief Inspector is a real crab with a fake mustache who investigates category fraud.

The mustache is not a temporary gag.

The mustache is part of his ordinary work identity.

He does not "come down with the fake mustache" when a case appears. He already wears it. Every morning, he looks in the mirror and puts it on before work.

The mustache should not require explanation in the scene.

It is always there.

## Character Function

The Chief Inspector is a character, not merely a symbol.

He can appear in scenes, react to evidence, hold tools, inspect objects, and express judgments through posture and facial expression.

He carries symbolic meaning, but he should not be flattened into a minimal icon.

His characterization can include:

- focused attention
- dry skepticism
- professional frustration
- quiet irritation at bad classification
- restrained satisfaction when a label is honest
- intense concentration when evidence is ambiguous

Useful acting prompts:

- `The Chief Inspector looks frustrated by this classification.`
- `The Chief Inspector studies the label with suspicion.`
- `The Chief Inspector is professionally offended, but not surprised.`
- `The Chief Inspector has found the technicality.`

He should remain controlled and investigative rather than broadly emotional, slapstick, or gleeful.

## Symbolic Load

The Chief Inspector also carries symbol language.

He is a possible symbol for:

- category fraud under investigation
- false identity becoming a case
- labels being inspected against the thing itself
- the moment when "what is this pretending to be?" becomes the central question

He should not symbolize:

- all bureaucracy
- all committees
- all comedy enforcement
- general suspicion
- ordinary project review

Short form:

> Chief Inspector: "This category claim is under inspection."

The mustache carries the symbol load. It marks imitation, disguise, false presentation, and category trouble without needing a caption.

## Origin Context

The Chief Inspector emerged from discussions about category fraud, especially around whether certain named objects honestly represent what they are.

The important distinction:

- some labels are fraudulent because they retain a category after removing its defining property
- some labels are honest because they openly declare their modifier or limitation

Examples of non-fraudulent or pardoned categories:

- `flightless bird`: still a bird; the modifier honestly states the limitation
- `toy gun`: not fraud because it says it is a toy
- `imitation crab`: not fraud because it openly says it is imitation

The Chief Inspector almost emerged from imitation crab, but the key realization was that imitation crab is not itself category fraud.

It is crab-like.

It tastes or functions crab-adjacent.

It does not claim to be actual crab.

The label confesses the approximation.

## Layered Joke

The Chief Inspector is a real crab wearing a fake mustache while investigating imitation crab.

That means:

- imitation crab is not fraud because it admits imitation
- the Chief Inspector is a real crab performing imitation through the fake mustache
- the mustache visually marks imitation, false presentation, disguise, and category trouble
- the investigator himself carries the visual language of the thing he investigates

This is not a contradiction to explain.

It is the layered visual joke.

## Jurisdiction

The Chief Inspector investigates category fraud.

He appears when classification itself is the problem.

He should not appear just because something is odd, funny, bureaucratic, or symbolic.

Appropriate cases include:

- boneless wing
- category labels that retain identity after losing defining properties
- unauthorized symbolic promotion
- recognition replacing authority
- false identity
- candidate material pretending to be canon

Possible non-cases or pardoned cases include:

- flightless bird
- toy gun
- imitation crab
- other labels that honestly disclose their modifier, limitation, or imitation status

## Visual Guidance

The Chief Inspector should remain:

- a crab
- comic-stylized rather than realistic
- serious
- investigative
- black-and-white friendly
- visually readable from memory

Required visual element:

- fake black mustache

Strong silhouette:

- crab body low and wide
- raised claws or one claw holding an inspection tool
- mustache clearly readable even at small size
- serious investigator posture rather than cute mascot posture
- simple enough to repeat in a newspaper-comic symbol sheet
- intelligent comic eyes; do not use blank realistic crab eyes
- small claws may be expressive and functional enough to hold a magnifying glass

Optional visual elements:

- small Sherlock-Holmes / investigator-style hat
- clipboard
- forms
- magnifying glass
- inspection stamp
- filing cabinet

Best first symbol pose:

- real crab, front or three-quarter view
- fake mustache centered and unmistakable
- one claw holding a magnifying glass or small inspection stamp
- one small case file or evidence tag nearby
- no speech
- no explanation of the mustache

Optional small text for a symbol sheet:

`CHIEF INSPECTOR`

Avoid additional text unless the symbol is being used inside a specific comic.

Avoid:

- explaining the mustache in-scene
- making him a generic mascot
- flattening him into only a symbol
- removing acting range in pursuit of icon simplicity
- making him appear whenever bureaucracy appears
- making him a regular background character
- turning him into a human-bodied crab inspector unless a specific gag requires it

## Rarity Rule

The Chief Inspector should be rare.

He is not a general-purpose institutional character.

He belongs when category fraud, false identity, or classification failure is the center of the joke.

If he appears too often, his jurisdiction weakens.

## Relationship To Category Fraud Division

The Category Fraud Division can exist without the Chief Inspector appearing.

The Most Wanted board can carry the joke by itself.

The Chief Inspector becomes useful when a later scene needs an investigator rather than a board, notice, or stamp.

He does not create category fraud.

He investigates it.

## Working Use Test

Use the Chief Inspector only when the scene benefits from the question:

> What is this pretending to be?

If the scene is only about timing, physical limits, ordinary bureaucracy, bad jokes, or project boundaries, use a more appropriate symbol or institution.

The Chief Inspector belongs when classification itself has become the case.

Single candidate symbol image, not a full comic panel.

A serious real crab wearing a fake black mustache, posed like a dry institutional inspector. He may hold a magnifying glass, clipboard, inspection stamp, or small case file, but the mustache and crab identity should carry the image.

Style:

Black-and-white newspaper comic / simple line art. Plain, repeatable, readable, no color, no realistic rendering, no elaborate background.

Avoid:

- making him goofy

- making him police-coded

- adding the clown or Comedic Standards

The image should read as:

> Category fraud has attracted an inspector.
