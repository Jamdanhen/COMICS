## Concept

A tiny living idea begins inside Taskmaster's thought bubble, transfers onto paper as a doodle, becomes present on the computer screen, and then quietly escapes from behind the screen into the wider workspace.

The page should communicate transference of an idea across forms:

Thought -> page -> screen -> process space.

The idea is not bad. It is alive, valuable, and not fully contained by any single medium.

## Intended Page Rules

- No visible title.
- No captions.
- No dialogue.
- No readable labels.
- Taskmaster only.
- The living idea blob has no mouth and no labels.
- The screen escape is the central visual beat.

## Draft Strengths

- The thought bubble, doodle, and screen transfer read clearly.
- The idea creature remains nonverbal.
- The fifth panel successfully shows tendrils emerging from behind the monitor.
- The page is much closer to symbolic transference than the previous literal evolution-chart draft.

## Review Notes

- The final panel may drift too far into process-machine territory for this first comic.
- The computer interface includes faint UI-like marks, but no strong readable text.
- If revised, consider keeping the final panel focused on tendrils escaping behind the screen rather than already reaching a full control panel.
- Consider whether the final panel should end with Taskmaster noticing one tendril crossing from the digital space into the physical workspace, instead of showing the larger process frame.
- The idea itself must visibly grow at each transfer. The joke does not work if only the tendrils grow while the screen/blob body remains the same size.
- On the page, the doodled blob should become larger between panels before it escapes the notebook.
- On the screen, the blob should also grow larger, so the screen containment feels temporary and insufficient.
- Growth should add structure, not just scale. As the blob grows, it should develop extra eyes, small appendages, surface bumps, depth, and irregular living form.
- Avoid making the blob look like a flat amoeba or a simple larger puddle.
- Reduce the number of tendrils. The screen escape should feel like stealthy overflow: a few exploratory tendrils slipping behind the monitor or into the desk/cables, not a full tentacle explosion.
- The blob should not appear to be intentionally grasping toward the next process step. It is escaping containment, not deliberately seeking entanglement or a new cage.
- Consider reducing the physical escape to one small first tendril emerging from the screen or behind the monitor.
- The screen-stage action may happen mostly inside the computer: the blob grows within the digital workspace and begins making recursive/spiral-like internal patterns that visually suggest review loops, redundancy, or distracting process recursion.
- If using this internal-screen behavior, avoid over-explaining it. The blob can appear to be arranging itself into a spiral or loop on the screen while one small tendril slips out unnoticed.
- A subtle Razorforge presence may be indicated by the computer/interface context or a small Razorforge-like symbol in the digital workspace, but do not introduce Razorforge as a full character in this draft unless Taskmaster asks.
- The screen should not make the blob look malignant. The idea is that the living information has learned to occupy the system and can generate loops, not that it is evil.
- Taskmaster is too slim in this draft. Future revisions must follow the locked visual calibration: sturdy, broad, moderate solid build; heavier than thin/fit, not bulky or exaggerated.

## Taskmaster Review - Panel 4 Correction

Most of the visual cues are working, and Panel 3's layout is a strong direction.

Panel 4 should not use a large swirling blob, vortex, or magical/RPG-looking effect. That changes the visual language too much and makes the transition feel like chaos or spellcasting.

The better Panel 4 action is the first transformation stage: the repeated page-drawings grow across the sheets, fill toward the central meeting corners of the pages, and begin to coalesce there. The forms should still be connected to the paper surface at first, like flat drawn information becoming dimensional.

Visual reference:

- Think of a liquid-metal coalescence effect, like the first stage of the T-1000 forming upward from a puddle in *Terminator 2*: not airborne chaos, but material pulling together into a vertical mass.
- The page corners meet or line up, and the blobbing mass gathers at that shared corner area.
- A few tendrils may begin to sprout upward, but they should be restrained and early-stage.
- Avoid little raised swirls, vortex marks, or arcane-looking shapes.
- Avoid making Panel 4 look like the image has already started over on the computer screen. The screen transition belongs later.

Next revision target:

Panel 4 should show a mostly paper-bound coalescence becoming three-dimensional: duplicated flat drawings grow into the central page-corner junction, merge into a small rising liquid mass, and only begin to lift from the paper.
